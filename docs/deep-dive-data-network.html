Data Network Compounder

A proprietary dataset is assembled — through a product that generates exhaust data, through acquisitions of data-producing businesses, or through aggregation of fragmented sources. Access is sold via API, dashboard, or licensed feed. The data compounds because more customers contribute more data, strengthening the position over time.

01Executive Summary

The archetype produces the strongest economics in the universe — high multiple, deep moat, moderate dilution — but only when a unique data position can be assembled before competitors.

How this archetype is defined in our framework

A proprietary dataset is assembled through one of three motions — exhaust from a primary product (Plaid, Snowflake), acquisition of data-producing businesses (Datavant, Verisk), or aggregation of fragmented public and contributed data (CoStar, MSCI) — and access to that dataset is sold via API, dashboard, or licensed feed. The dataset compounds because more customers and integrations contribute more data, deepening coverage, freshness, and linkage that strengthen the position over time and raise switching cost for the buyer. What the customer actually pays for is decision-quality information at the moment of use: coverage (the universe of records the dataset spans), freshness (how recently updated), linkage (the ability to join across silos), and trust (regulatory, fiduciary, and legal acceptance of the data as the reference). The defining structural fact is that, once assembled, the dataset is largely a fixed cost — incremental margins approach 90%, EBITDA margins for mature operators reach 50–55%, NRR runs 125–160% on consumption pricing. The two compounding asymmetries — time on supply (integrations take years to build, antitrust now blocks consolidation as a shortcut) and switching cost on demand (customers build models, regulatory filings, and downstream products on top of your schema) — produce multi-decade defensibility when combined with a regulatory or trust moat. Quantitatively: exit multiples 10–25× ARR, capital required $50–200M for pure SaaS; $250M–$1B+ for acquisition-led, founder ownership at exit 10–35%, time horizon 6–10 years to meaningful exit. The combined enabler-fit score is 48 out of 125 — high AI fit, partial SMB fit (acquisition as data-assembly tactic, not customer base).

02The Logic — Pyramid Decomposition

Compounding occurs at three layers: data position asymmetry at the apex, integration cost at the middle, switching-cost lock-in at the base.

Data Network Compounder — Multi-Decade Defensibility │ ├── Apex · Unique Data Position (irreplicable for >5 years) │ ├── Coverage — the universe of records the dataset spans │ ├── Freshness — minutes for market data; days for credit; real-time for connectivity │ ├── Linkage — ability to join across silos (Datavant tokens, MSCI index chain) │ └── Trust — regulatory, legal, fiduciary acceptance │ ├── Middle · Replication Engine (integrations take years to build) │ ├── Source acquisition: bank deals, MLS feeds, hospital data agreements │ ├── Normalisation: schema unification, entity resolution, deduplication │ ├── Regulatory positioning: HIPAA Safe Harbor, GLBA, FCRA, GDPR │ └── Linkage layer: tokens / IDs / keys joining records across silos │ └── Base · Customer Switching Cost (the compounding mechanism) ├── Customers build models on top of your schema ├── Customers cite your data in regulatory filings (MSCI in ETF prospectuses) ├── Replacing you requires rebuilding internal workflows + downstream products └── NRR 125–160% in mature operators (Snowflake 131%)
Reading the pyramid

The two compounding asymmetries — time on supply (integrations require years) and switching cost on demand (rebuilding customer workflows requires more years) — combined with a regulatory or trust moat produce the strongest defensibility in the universe of games. Where either is missing, the business reverts to commodity data resale where gross margins collapse below 50% and NRR falls below 105%.

03Business Model

The customer pays for decision-quality information at the moment needed — coverage, freshness, linkage, trust — delivered via API, dashboard, or licensed feed.

What the customer pays for

Buyers are not purchasing raw bytes — they are purchasing coverage (the universe of records the dataset spans: every US bank, every CRE building, every public equity, every claims record), freshness (how recently updated — minutes for market data, days/weeks for credit, real-time for connectivity APIs), linkage (the ability to join this dataset to others — Datavant tokens, Plaid's account-to-identity match, MSCI's index → constituents → risk-factor chain), and trust (regulatory, legal, fiduciary acceptance — Verisk's ISO data is what state insurance regulators expect; MSCI indices are what pension trustees must benchmark to).

How the dataset is assembled — three motions

  • Exhaust from a primary product (Plaid: account-linking exhaust → identity, balance, transactions products; Snowflake: workload exhaust → Data Cloud marketplace).
  • Acquisition of data-producing businesses (Datavant + Ciox 2021; Verisk's ~70 acquisitions over two decades).
  • Aggregation of fragmented public/contributed data (CoStar's field researchers physically inspecting buildings; MSCI's index constituent collection).

Three representative compounders

  • Plaid. Connectivity API for 12,000+ US/CA/EU financial institutions; ~500M consumer accounts connected. Revenue $390M (2024) → ~$546M ARR (2025) per Sacra; 40% YoY growth.   • Snowflake. Cloud data warehouse + Data Cloud Marketplace where third parties license curated datasets to Snowflake customers in-place. FY24 product revenue $2.67B; NRR 131%. Pricing consumption credits. Network effect: marketplace flywheel — more customers attract more data providers (Bloomberg, Experian, Weather Co.), which deepens the consumption stack. IPO Sept 2020 at $33B / day-1 close $70B; Frank Slootman held 5.9% per S-1.
  • Verisk. Insurance data, modelling, analytics. Dominant in P&C ratemaking via the ISO data pool. Revenue $2.88B FY24 (+7.5% YoY). Adjusted EBITDA margin 54.7% — among the highest in B2B data. Pricing multi-year (1–5 year) subscriptions paid quarterly or upfront. Network effect: insurers contribute loss data into ISO pool → Verisk produces rate filings, fraud models, cat models → carriers buy back as licensed product to defend rate filings before state insurance commissioners. Regulatory entrenchment + contributor flywheel.

04The Replication Unit

The atomic unit is the data integration — the new bank pipe, hospital data-sharing agreement, MLS feed, or exchange data licence — not the customer.

What gets replicated

The unit that scales is not the customer (which is the SaaS unit) — it is the data integration: the new bank pipe, the new hospital data-sharing agreement, the new MLS feed, the new exchange data licence, the new state insurance commission filing. Each integration unlocks (a) data depth for existing customers and (b) coverage that makes the product viable for a new customer segment.

Cost to deploy one more unit

  • Plaid bank integration: legal review of bank's terms; engineering to handle bank's auth flow (OAuth where available, screen-scraping fallback); QA + monitoring; ongoing maintenance when bank UI changes. Industry-cited cost is six-figure all-in per major bank initially, declining to mid-five-figures as playbook matures. Large banks now require formal commercial agreements (the JPMorgan / Plaid / Coinbase fee dispute of 2025 reflected this shift).
  • Datavant partner: HIPAA review, tokenisation deployment at partner site, data quality validation, ongoing pipeline ops — multi-quarter.
  • CoStar building: physical inspection by field researcher + ongoing telephone refresh — pure labour cost.
  • MSCI index methodology: research-team time to design and maintain; licensing to ETF issuers is high-leverage.

Diseconomies of scale

  • Source-side power. Once Plaid integrates JPMorgan, JPMorgan can demand pricing concessions or impose access restrictions — a contributor that becomes a gatekeeper. JPMorgan's reported 2025 moves to charge data aggregators for API access illustrate this risk.
  • Customer concentration in regulated verticals. Equifax/Experian/TransUnion serve a small number of giant lenders; pricing pressure compounds at scale.
  • Long-tail integration maintenance. At 12,000 bank integrations, the marginal new bank serves <0.001% of users but still requires ongoing engineering. Plaid has reported significant ops cost in maintaining low-traffic integrations as bank UIs change.

COGS structure

  • Hosting and infrastructure (8–15% of revenue for most data businesses; Snowflake passes through cloud-vendor COGS).
  • Data licensing fees paid out (Bloomberg pays exchanges; MSCI pays index licensors; Plaid increasingly pays large banks for API access).

NRR is structurally elevated because (a) consumption-based pricing scales with customer data growth, (b) more datasets in marketplace = more upsell, (c) datasets are sticky to internal models built on them.

CAC and payback

  • PLG-leaning data APIs (Plaid, early Snowflake): CAC payback 12–18 months at scale; bottom-up adoption reduces sales cost.
  • Enterprise field sales (MSCI, Verisk, Bloomberg, CoStar): CAC payback 18–30 months for new logo; <12 months for expansion in same logo.
  • LTV/CAC in mature operators routinely exceeds 5× because NRR compounds while CAC stays largely on new-logo motion.

Network-effect mechanics — the differentiator

  • 1. More customers → more data contribution → better product for all customers. Verisk's ISO pool is the cleanest example: each member carrier contributes loss data; resulting actuarial tables are more accurate; each member benefits more than a non-member.
  • 2. More integrations → more coverage → more customers viable. Plaid: once 99% of US deposit accounts are reachable, any fintech can serve any user, making Plaid the default choice.
  • 3. Marketplace effects. Snowflake Data Cloud: more customers attract more data providers (Bloomberg, Experian, Weather Co. list on Snowflake Marketplace), deepening the consumption stack.
  • 4. Identifier / linkage effects. Datavant tokens: once the industry standardises on Datavant's tokenisation for trial-to-RWD linkage, any new data partner that does not tokenise the same way is unusable.
  • 5. Regulatory entrenchment. MSCI's indices are written into pension-fund mandates; replacing MSCI requires LP votes and prospectus updates — far higher friction than switching CRMs.

  • Enterprise field sales with data-scientist sales engineers. MSCI, Verisk, Bloomberg, CoStar, Datavant all run this — 12–24 month new-logo cycles, multi-year contracts, account managers per account.
  • Developer-led / PLG for API products. Plaid in its early years was almost entirely developer-driven (sandbox → free tier → metered overages → enterprise). Modern Snowflake mid-market adoption follows a similar arc.

SMB acquisition as data-acquisition tactic

Verisk's two-decade run of ~70 acquisitions used M&A as a primary data-acquisition method — buying specialty analytics shops (Argus, Wood Mackenzie energy, Maplecroft political risk) for their data, talent, customer relationships. Datavant's $7B merger with Ciox in 2021 was explicitly data-acquisition: Ciox brought the medical-records request network, Datavant brought the tokenisation linkage layer. The pattern: buy when (a) the target controls a data source you cannot build, (b) the target has regulatory permission or customer trust you cannot replicate, or (c) the target's customer relationships shorten your data-gathering timeline by years.

07Capital Structure and Founder Economics

Capital required $5–15M for pure SaaS data API, $50–250M+ for acquisition-led plays; founders typically retain 10–20% at IPO; antitrust now forces standalone IPO.

Capital required to start

  • Pure SaaS data API: $5–15M seed-Series A funds product, first integrations, design partners.
  • Acquisition-led data play: $50–250M Series B/C+ needed before first scale acquisition, because the target's revenue needs to be paid for.

08Risks and Failure Modes

Seven recurring failure modes: data breach, customer disintermediation, regulatory exposure, source-side power shift, antitrust block, technology disruption, single-customer concentration.

  • 1. Data breach destroys trust and triggers regulatory penalties. Equifax (2017): 143M consumers exposed, cumulative cost >$1.4B including $700M settlement with FTC/CFPB/states. Reputational scar lasts a decade; competitors gain pricing room.
  • 2. Customer disintermediation. When data customers reach scale, they build internal alternatives. Large quant funds replaced Bloomberg modules with internal pipelines; large insurers built proprietary data assets to reduce ISO dependence; Goldman/Morgan Stanley historically considered building Bloomberg substitutes.
  • 3. Regulatory exposure. FCRA (credit data), HIPAA (health data), GLBA (financial privacy), GDPR/state privacy laws (consumer data), MiFID II (market data). A single enforcement action can wipe out a category.
  • 4. Source-side power shift. When the data supplier becomes a gatekeeper, they extract rents. JPMorgan's 2025 moves to charge aggregators for API access; LSE/ICE/CME exchange-data fee increases consistently outpacing inflation.
  • 5. Antitrust block of consolidation path. Plaid-Visa 2020 set the precedent; subsequent deals (S&P-IHS Markit closed only after divestitures; LSEG-Refinitiv required EU concessions) face heightened scrutiny.
  • 6. Technology disruption around the incumbent. Open Banking regulations (UK PSD2, EU PSD3, US 1033) commoditise bank connectivity; alternative-data startups (FICO challengers using cash-flow data, climate-risk modellers attacking Verisk cat models) attack incumbents from new data angles.
  • 7. Single-customer concentration. Common in early-stage; FactSet's early dependence on a few asset managers; Datavant's life-sciences concentration risk.

Documented historical examples

  • Equifax 2017 breach: $1.4B+ cumulative cost, 60%+ stock decline at trough, CEO/CIO/CSO departures, multi-year regulatory consent orders.
  • Plaid-Visa antitrust block 2020: forced Plaid back to standalone path; ultimately turned out fine (now ~$8B IPO-track) but cost ~2 years.
  • Bloomberg "spy" scandal 2013: revelation that Bloomberg journalists could see client activity led to backlash from Goldman, JPMorgan; required structural separation of editorial and data.
  • Dun & Bradstreet: serial restructuring, taken private (2018, $5.4B), re-IPO'd (2020), underperformed — illustrates difficulty when data freshness lags and customer alternatives proliferate.
  • Symphony Communications challenge to Bloomberg messaging: 13-bank consortium funded alternative; took share at the margin but did not unseat Terminal.

Preconditions for the archetype to work

  • Genuinely unique data position (irreplicable for >5 years).
  • Regulatory or operational moat sustaining that uniqueness.
  • Capital to fund the integration / acquisition phase (typically $50M+).
  • Anchor customers willing to pay before the dataset is complete.
  • Team capable of operating compliance and security at financial-grade.
Sources: Bitdefender summary of Equifax breach cost; PBS NewsHour Equifax $700M settlement; CNBC on Visa-Plaid; Goodwin client alert on Visa-Plaid antitrust.
09When This Game Is Playable

The game requires data engineering at scale, regulatory leadership, and the right two compounding asymmetries — time on supply and switching cost on demand.

Required team capabilities

  • Data engineering at scale: distributed pipelines, entity resolution, schema evolution. The hardest hires.
  • Regulatory and compliance leadership: ex-FTC, ex-CFPB, ex-OCC, ex-HHS-OCR, ex-state-insurance-commission backgrounds dominate this role at top operators.
  • Enterprise sales with data fluency: sales engineers fluent in SQL, data models, regulator-facing rate filings.
  • Partnership / BD: closes the data-supply side — bank deals, hospital agreements, exchange feeds.
  • Security: financial-grade infosec (SOC 2 Type II, ISO 27001, HITRUST for health data) is table stakes.
  • Product: ability to package the same dataset into multiple product SKUs for different buyer personas.

Required capital base

  • $10–50M to validate position: assemble first 3–5 data partners, ship MVP, sign 5–10 design-partner customers.
  • $50–250M to reach $50M ARR: fund enterprise GTM and integration backlog.
  • $250M–$1B to reach IPO-ready scale in pure-organic mode; more if M&A-led.
  • Acquisition-led plays require an additional $100M–$1B+ M&A war chest because data assets are typically priced at 8–15× revenue.

Required market conditions

  • Data scarcity in target domain (not commoditised public data).
  • Willingness-to-pay for proprietary data above $50K/year minimum — sub-$50K markets cannot fund the integration cost structure.
  • Regulatory clarity sufficient for enterprise customers to procure (HIPAA Safe Harbor framework, FCRA permissible purpose, GLBA compliance pathways must exist).
  • Customer base with repeated use cases that drive recurring spend rather than one-shot data purchases.
  • At least one durable supply-side source of advantage: contributor pool, exclusive licence, regulator-approved methodology, or platform integration.

Required time horizon

  • 6–10 years to meaningful exit is the empirical norm.
  • Plaid: founded 2013, ~12 years to IPO track in 2026 (interrupted by Visa block).
  • Snowflake: founded 2012, IPO 2020 (8 years).
  • Datavant: founded 2017, $7B merger 2021 (4 years — fast, because acquisition-led).
  • Verisk: founded 1971 as ISO; IPO 2009 (38 years — pre-cloud era).
  • MSCI: spun from Morgan Stanley 2007 after decades of internal incubation.

Disqualifying conditions

  • Data is already commoditised: weather data (post-NOAA opening), public company filings (post-SEC EDGAR), basic geographic data (post-OpenStreetMap).
  • No unique position assembly path: if any well-capitalised competitor can replicate the dataset in <12 months, no compounding moat forms.
  • Regulatory uncertainty making enterprise procurement impossible: this is why ad-tech data brokers struggle to scale enterprise contracts as state privacy laws fragment.
  • Sub-scale willingness-to-pay: SMB-only markets that cannot bear $50K+ contracts cannot fund the integration backlog.
  • Supplier power concentration before you have scale: trying to build a "Plaid for X" when X has 3 dominant data sources who will each demand exclusivity or rents.
The structural arbitrage

The archetype's defensibility comes from two compounding asymmetries. Time asymmetry on supply: integrations take years to negotiate and engineer. A new entrant cannot catch up by spending money faster, only by waiting (or by acquiring an incumbent — increasingly blocked by antitrust). Switching-cost asymmetry on demand: once customers build internal workflows, models, regulatory filings, and downstream products on top of your data schema and identifiers, replacing you requires rebuilding all of it. NRR of 125–170% is the financial signature of this switching cost. Where these two asymmetries combine with a regulatory or trust moat — Verisk's regulator-approved actuarial methodology, MSCI's ETF benchmarks in LP mandates, Datavant's HIPAA tokenisation, Plaid's bank-side trust — the result is a multi-decade compounder. Where either is missing, the business reverts to commodity data resale.
