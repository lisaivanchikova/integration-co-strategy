Pure HoldCo Positioning — Why We Are Not a Services Firm

A structured decision memo explaining: (a) what game we chose to play, (b) what game we deliberately did not choose, (c) why the "hybrid services + acquisitions" path that initially looked attractive structurally destroys the model, (d) what our position actually is — and what it allows us to do.

01The Question

Among three structurally different paths, which game do we play — and what does the choice forbid us to do?

The Studio was originally framed as a three-entity structure: Fund (capital) + AI-Integration Co (sells AI services to third-party SMEs) + Holding Company (acquires SMEs in same vertical). Independent analysis through the Game Selection Framework and four archetype deep dives concluded that the highest-fit model on our three enabling capabilities (US capital markets, SMB acquisition, AI deployment) is AI-Native Operating Holdco. This memo answers: what does choosing that game require us to stop doing, what does it allow us to keep doing, and how do we name the position we now occupy.

Bottom Line Up Front

We abandoned the path of building a standing AI services firm that sells to a broad market of third-party SMEs. We did not abandon the practice of running paid pilots with one to two operator-partners per vertical as a validation gate before acquisition. The first is a different business model with structurally lower outcomes. The second is the L2 mechanic inside the AI-Native Operating Holdco model and matches what every documented operator in the category already does. Our position is therefore "AI-Native Operating Holdco with paid pre-acquisition pilots" — and the distinction between "pilot" and "services arm" is the entire memo.

02The Three Paths Available

Three structurally distinct business models are available to a team with our three enabling capabilities; they are not blendable.

Path A · Rejected as primary model

Services-First Operator (the Distyl path)

Build a standing AI services firm that sells implementation, integration, and managed AI products to a broad market of customers. Recurring revenue from N clients. Never acquires any customer.

Reference operators: Distyl AI ($1.8B valuation on $31M ARR), Sierra ($15.8B), Harvey ($11B), Accenture, BCG-X, McKinsey QuantumBlack, Bain.

Why rejected: exit multiple is 1–3× EBITDA on the services line (private market) or 4–8× ARR for SaaS-classified services. Founder cash-out potential $50–120M. Our $52M capital base (S16 + co-investors) is structurally HoldCo-grade, not bootstrap-grade for a services firm. Capability mismatch.

Not our game
Path B · Initially attractive, structurally broken

Hybrid — Standing Services Firm + Same-Vertical Acquisitions (the "Empty Quadrant")

Run a standing AI services firm selling to many third-party SMEs in a vertical, while in parallel acquiring some of those (or adjacent) SMEs and rolling them up. The "empty quadrant" position on the original 2×2 matrix.

Reference operators: None documented at our scale. The position is empty because every attempted hybrid collapsed on at least one of five structural points (analysis in Section 04).

Why rejected: not because the position is novel, but because the structural mechanics that make Path B attractive on paper are exactly the mechanics that destroy enterprise value at exit.

Rejected — see Section 04
Path C · Chosen

AI-Native Operating Holdco with Paid Pre-Acquisition Pilots

Acquire operating SMBs in fragmented verticals, install AI inside, hold as decentralised opcos under a holding company. AI engineering arm housed inside the HoldCo. Before each acquisition, run a paid pilot with one to two operator-partners in the vertical to validate ≥1.5× productivity uplift on real client data. The operator pays the pilot fee; the Studio commits acquisition capital only if the gate clears.

Reference operators: Long Lake ($1.28B raised), GC Creation ($1.5B), Shield Technology Partners ($200M, nine MSPs), Crete/Current ($428M revenue), Smarter Tech/NMC ($1B+). All five run paid pre-acquisition pilots; none runs a standing services arm.

Why chosen: uses all three enabling capabilities at structural strength (enabler-fit score 100/125, highest of fourteen archetypes), multiple arbitrage 3–6× SMB → 8–14× platform exit, founder cash-out $200–400M, 5 documented operator references.

03The Evaluation Framework

A model is judged on five structural questions; Path B fails four of the five and Path C passes all five.

Every business model that combines services revenue with acquisition revenue must answer five structural questions. The answers determine whether the model survives to exit at the multiple its components imply. Path B and Path C answer these questions differently — the questions are not negotiable and the answers are not interchangeable.

04Why Path B Breaks the Model — Five Concrete Mechanisms

Path B fails not because it is novel but because five independent structural mechanisms each, on their own, are sufficient to destroy the value the model was built to capture.

Mechanism 1 — Multiple compression on consolidated exit

Public and private market data on sector multiples (Damodaran NYU Stern, January 2026 update; PitchBook Q4 2025):

Revenue categoryPrivate multiple rangeWhy this multiple
AI-integrated SMB roll-up (post-AI uplift)8–14× EBITDABuyer pays for verified margin uplift + scaled platform
Services / consulting boutique1–3× EBITDABuyer applies services multiple — human capital, no recurring software lock-in
Hot AI-services peak (rare)1× revenue (Distyl)Strategic acquirer in narrow window; not repeatable for SMB-services

On our numbers: if a Path B HoldCo arrives at exit with $50M EBITDA where $35M is roll-up margin and $15M is services revenue, the strategic buyer will not pay 12× on the $50M ($600M EV). The buyer will compute mix-weighted multiple: 8× × ($35M ÷ $50M) + 2× × ($15M ÷ $50M) ≈ 6.2× blended → $310M EV. Lost enterprise value: ~$290M. The mechanism is documented in Smarter Technologies / NMC: New Mountain Capital combined SmarterDx (SaaS) + Access Healthcare (services) + Thoughtful.ai (AI) into one entity; CEO Delinsky stepped down via LinkedIn after 7 months (December 2025); public reporting (Becker's Hospital Review, Modern Healthcare) frames the failure as integration of services revenue with platform revenue under acquisition-target time pressure.

Mechanism 2 — Section 206 conflict of interest with future targets

SEC Section 206(3) requires registered investment advisers to obtain client consent before engaging in principal transactions with affiliates. When the same Studio sells AI services to a customer and later considers acquiring that customer, every transaction history becomes a disclosure item. Documented enforcement settlements 2016–2025: Apollo Global $52.7M (2016, inadequate disclosure of monitoring fees), Energy Capital Partners settled (2022), Insight Venture Management settled (2023), TZP Group $500K+ (2025, fee-offset double-counting). Sources: SEC press releases, Sidley TZP analysis (August 2025). Disclosure complexity is the documented failure mode, not the theoretical risk.

Mechanism 3 — Bargaining position erosion at acquisition

Selling AI services to 10 HR agencies in a vertical does two things to acquisition negotiations: (a) leaks our internal economics — every prospective target's CFO knows our pricing, margin structure, and engineering cost from product invoices; (b) creates 9 informed competitors of the 10th company we eventually buy, with detailed knowledge of our AI stack and operational rhythm. In a Pure HoldCo + private pilot model, the operator-partner signs an NDA before the pilot and the rest of the vertical learns nothing about our economics.

Mechanism 4 — Engineering bandwidth split

Documented engineering headcount ratios from comparable operators:

  • Sierra (pure services-first): ~50+ engineers for $150M ARR. Engineering scales linearly with logo count, customer success, and white-glove implementations. Source: TechCrunch reporting on Sierra Series E (May 2026).
  • Long Lake (pure HoldCo): ~5 forward-deployed engineers supporting $1.28B raised and 30 acquisitions. Engineering scales with acquisition count, not customer count. Source: General Catalyst "Future of Services" essay (2026), PitchBook profile.

Path B requires both teams operating in parallel — services scaling team to support growing customer base + acquisition deployment team to integrate acquired OpCos. Two teams of different dynamics, different management, different career paths. $52M Fund cannot fund both at meaningful scale. Forcing the same team to do both produces under-served customers (churn) or under-integrated acquisitions (margin uplift fails).

Mechanism 5 — LP disclosure burden

Path B requires the PPM to disclose (i) AI-Integration Co fee structure, (ii) related-party pricing methodology, (iii) Section 206(3) mitigations including arms-length pricing with annual third-party benchmark, (iv) audit trail for every cross-entity transaction, (v) management fee offset mechanism. The disclosure infrastructure investment is $50–100K upfront and 0.5 FTE perpetual compliance. Documented enforcement settlements show that disclosure execution failures, not structure choice, drive penalties (Sidley analysis, August 2025). Path C eliminates the disclosure surface entirely: operator at pilot stage is not yet portfolio company, Fund is not yet counterparty, no related-party transactions occur.

Compound mechanism — not five independent risks

The five mechanisms are not five independent risks of which one might trigger. They are five facets of a single structural fact: combining services revenue with acquisition revenue under one entity converts every relationship in the business from cooperative to adversarial — customer becomes future negotiation counterparty, regulator becomes ongoing audit risk, LP becomes governance complexity, engineering becomes bandwidth conflict. Each mechanism amplifies the others.

05Why Path C Works — What Pre-Acquisition Pilots Actually Do

Paid pilots are not "selling services" — they are a documented L2 validation gate that every reference operator in the AI-Native HoldCo category runs.

The mechanic that confuses pilot with services arm is the fact that money flows from operator to Studio. The structural difference between pilot and services is not whether money flows, but what the money buys and what happens at the end of the engagement.

Paid pilot — inside our model

  • Duration: 60–90 days, time-bounded
  • Counterparty: 1–2 operator-partners per vertical, NDA-protected
  • Fee structure: one-off $100–300K covering AI Engineering loaded cost
  • Purpose: validate ≥1.5× productivity uplift on real client data
  • End state: acquire operator's company OR walk away
  • P&L classification: acquisition diligence cost recovered from target — not recurring services revenue
  • Conferences: sourcing operator-partners for L1 stage of the funnel

Standing services arm — breaks the model

  • Duration: ongoing, indefinite
  • Counterparty: N customers in open market, multiple per vertical
  • Fee structure: recurring monthly/annual subscription
  • Purpose: generate recurring revenue stream
  • End state: customer remains customer indefinitely
  • P&L classification: services revenue line item — consolidated multiple compression at exit
  • Conferences: lead generation for services subscription pipeline

Documented practice at reference operators

Every operator in our reference set runs paid pre-acquisition engagements with operator-partners; none runs a standing external services arm. Selected evidence:

  • Long Lake. Each new vertical begins with an operator-partner engagement before HoldCo formation. AI deployment time compressed from over 12 months on early deals to days on recent deals in same vertical — the engagement is documented by General Catalyst as the validation phase, not a services line. Source: GC "Future of Services" essay (2026).
  • Shield Technology Partners. MSP acquisitions structured at 60–90% stake, sellers retain leadership + local brand. Prior to acquisition Shield engages operator candidates on shared infrastructure (Sentinel, Spectre) — but does not sell these products externally to non-portfolio MSPs. Source: BusinessWire (June 2025), Channel Insider, MSP Summit.
  • Crete / Current. Tax AI deployed inside acquired accounting firms, achieves 31% time savings + 98% accuracy on 7,000 returns. Tax AI is not sold as standalone SaaS to non-portfolio CPA firms. Source: BusinessWire rebrand announcement (June 2026), CPA Practice Advisor, OpenAI/Thrive joint press materials.
  • GC Creation. $1.5B vehicle inside Fund XII led by MD Marc Bhargava. Creation NewCos run as portfolio companies with shared AI engineering; AI products are not licensed to operators outside the Creation portfolio. Source: Sourcery VC analysis, MobiHealthNews.

Why this matches our existing practice

The Studio's current activities are entirely inside Path C:

  • Lusin × Glocap (HR Staffing, NY). Paid pilot with one operator-partner (Adam Zoia / Glocap) inside their live workflow. Validation target ≥1.5×. Acquisition discussion built into engagement. Single operator per vertical at pilot stage. Matches Path C exactly.
  • HomeCare / Allan Gobbs / YCare. Parallel pilot, single operator-partner per vertical, paid validation engagement, acquisition discussion built in.
  • Property Management / Sergey. Config B variant — operator brings own software stack; HoldCo acquires the platform and folds software into portfolio.
  • Conference attendance. Sourcing operator-partners in target verticals (Hell-Yes / Strong scores on V×C×A×B×M map). Not lead-gen for services subscription.
The Studio is already running Path C

Every existing engagement is consistent with Path C and inconsistent with Path B. The confusion arose from text that said "AI not sold externally" without explaining that paid pilots with operator-partners are a documented part of the model. Path C is what we are doing. The Lusin × Glocap pilot, the YCare engagement, the conferences — all of these are correct and proceed unchanged.

06Final Positioning Statement

"AI-Native Operating Holdco with paid pre-acquisition pilots" — a defined position with explicit rules of what we do and what we deliberately do not do.

What this position allows — and what it forbids

Allowed under our model

  • Conferences attended for sourcing operator-partners in target verticals
  • Paid pilots with 1–2 operator-partners per vertical at any given time
  • Pilot fees $100–300K covering AI Engineering loaded cost
  • NDA-protected validation engagements 60–90 days
  • Acquisition discussion built into pilot terms from day one
  • Walk-away at end of pilot if ≥1.5× metric fails
  • Acquisition of operator's company if metric clears
  • Post-acquisition deployment of validated AI stack across HoldCo OpCos and all subsequent tuck-in acquisitions

Forbidden under our model

  • Standing AI subscription product sold to non-portfolio customers
  • Recurring monthly revenue from customers without acquisition intent
  • More than 2 simultaneous pilots per vertical (creates competitive intelligence leak)
  • Pilots that have no defined acquisition decision point
  • Lead-generation conferences for services subscription pipeline
  • Licensing AI products to operators outside our acquisition pipeline
  • Marketing the AI product as a standalone brand external to the HoldCo
  • Open-ended customer relationships beyond the pilot window

How to describe the Studio externally

Recommended external positioning statement

"We are an AI-Native Operating Holdco. We acquire operating SMBs in fragmented US service verticals — homecare, MSPs, accounting, P&C insurance brokerages, property management — and install AI inside them to raise margin and productivity. Before each acquisition, we run a paid pilot with the operator-partner to validate productivity uplift on their live workflow. We do not sell AI as a standalone product to companies we are not acquiring."

Why the distinction matters for the team

The same activity (operator pays the Studio, AI Engineering team builds inside their workflow) is "pilot" or "services" depending on (a) how many operators per vertical we engage, (b) whether acquisition is on the table from day one, (c) what happens at the end. Practical operating rule: if at any point the team starts pursuing a third operator-partner in the same vertical at the same time, that is the moment we have drifted from Path C into Path B — and we should stop and reconsolidate.

What changes in the documents

Operating Model v4.0 and the Business Models doc both refer to "AI not sold externally" in language that proved too categorical. Three targeted edits to address:

  • v4.0 Investment Thesis lead: replace "AI products are deployed exclusively across acquired portfolio OpCos; they are not sold externally" with "AI products are deployed across acquired portfolio OpCos and inside operator-partners during paid pilots leading to acquisition; AI is not sold as a standalone subscription product to customers without acquisition intent".
  • Business Models doc Section 04 (card "What we do not do"): replace "License AI products to operators outside our portfolio" with "License AI products as standalone subscription business or to operators outside our acquisition pipeline".
  • Business Models doc Section 05 (reference operators table caption): add explicit note that all reference operators run paid pre-acquisition engagements with operator-partners — this is consistent with our position.
This memo is the source document for the three edits above. All three edits preserve the pure-HoldCo posture while making explicit that paid pre-acquisition pilots are part of the documented model, not a deviation from it.

Operating rule for the team

A "yes" on any single question to the bottom branch is enough to flag the activity for review. The model survives at exit only if all five questions answer to the top branch consistently across all activities. The single most actionable indicator is Q1: if we are pursuing three operators in the same vertical at the same time, we have left the model.
