Horizontal AI Agent Platform

A single technology base — foundation model, agent framework, or orchestration layer — sold as agentic AI products to enterprises across many industries. This document maps the business model, replication unit, unit economics, and go-to-market mechanics through the lens of the operators currently defining the category.

01Executive Summary

The archetype produces the largest absolute enterprise values in software but extracts the smallest founder ownership share in the universe of games.

How this archetype is defined in our framework

A single technology base — foundation model, agent framework, or orchestration layer — is sold as agentic AI products to enterprises across multiple industries from one common code path. The category spans two sub-types under the same economics: frontier model labs that produce the underlying foundation models (Anthropic, OpenAI) and sell tokenised inference via API, and application-layer agent platforms (Sierra, Decagon, Glean, Harvey, Cresta) that wrap a foundation-model API in a vertical-agnostic orchestration layer and sell agentic products to enterprises. What unifies the archetype is horizontality — the same product serves customer service at one buyer, sales development at another, legal drafting at a third — and the resulting market narrative of foundational technology with unbounded TAM, which justifies the highest revenue multiples in software. Quantitatively: exit multiples 15–40× ARR (50–100× at the model-lab tier), capital required to reach $100M ARR $200M–$20B+, founder ownership at exit 4–15%, scalability ceiling $10B–$100B+. The combined enabler-fit score against US-market, SMB-acquisition, and AI-deployment capabilities is 20 out of 125 — high on AI fit, low on SMB fit, because the customer is the enterprise not the SMB owner.

Bottom Line Up Front

The horizontal AI agent platform sells units of cognitive work priced against human labour cost rather than software seats. Frontier model labs (Anthropic, OpenAI) and application-layer agent platforms (Sierra, Decagon, Glean, Harvey, Cresta) clear 15–40× ARR multiples in private rounds, ten times the SaaS norm, but the capital required to reach scale produces founder dilution to single digits. The defining structural fact is the inference tax — AI-native gross margins average 52% versus 75–85% for mature SaaS, a 23–33 point compression that recovers only with hyperscaler distribution partnerships and proprietary model efficiency gains.

Finding 01
Frontier ARR growth is unprecedented but valuation absorbs the founder's share.
Anthropic went from $9B ARR end-2025 to $47B annualised in May 2026; Series H closed at $965B post-money. Sierra reached $150M ARR in 30 months at $15.8B valuation. Founder ownership at this scale typically clears under 10%, often under 5%.
Finding 02
Only 11–14% of enterprise AI agent pilots reach production at scale.
IDC reports 4 of 33 prototypes graduate. Failure modes: reliability degrades at production volume (3% pilot error becomes 300 weekly incidents at 10K volume), governance built post-hoc, unit economics flip when token cost exceeds human cost-per-task.
Finding 03
The reverse acqui-hire is the modal failure exit.
Adept ($400M raised → Amazon talent absorption), Inflection ($1.5B+ raised → Microsoft $650M payment + leadership), Character.AI (Google licensing deal). Investors recover 1–1.5× capital; founders cap out via talent absorption rather than equity outcome.

02The Logic — Pyramid Decomposition

Founder value in this archetype is the product of multiple expansion at the apex, capital concentration in the middle, and structural inference cost compression at the base.

Horizontal AI Agent Platform — Founder Value at Exit │ ├── Apex · Multiple (the highest in software economics) │ ├── Model lab: 50–100×+ ARR (Anthropic, OpenAI) │ ├── Application-layer: 15–40× ARR (Sierra, Harvey) │ └── Justified by narrative — foundational technology, unbounded TAM │ ├── Middle · Scale Engine (what converts capital into ARR) │ ├── Inference COGS: 8–23% of revenue depending on pricing model │ ├── Customer success delivery: 4–10 week onboarding at enterprise tier │ ├── Net revenue retention: 130–160% top quartile when usage-based │ └── Channel mix: hyperscaler marketplace + direct enterprise + PLG │ └── Base · Ownership Erosion (capital intensity dictates the founder's share) ├── Capital required to $100M ARR: $200–600M application; $5–20B+ model lab ├── Series-by-series dilution: 75–90% by Series E ├── Founder retained ownership at exit: 4–15% application; 0–8% model lab └── Exit absolute dollars high, percentage low
Reading the pyramid

The apex compensates for the base only when enterprise value reaches the multi-billion range. A $1B exit at 6% founder ownership produces $60M; a $50B exit at 6% produces $3B. The archetype is therefore a winner-take-most game — founders who hit the top tier extract life-changing dollars, but the median outcome is the reverse acqui-hire at 1–1.5× capital returned to investors and limited founder upside.

03Business Model

The customer buys units of cognitive work; the operator produces an inference engine, an orchestration layer, or both.

What the customer pays for

Buyers in this category are not purchasing software seats; they are purchasing a contractually-defined unit of cognitive work — a resolved support ticket (Sierra, Decagon), a drafted contract clause (Harvey), a retrieved-and-synthesised answer (Glean), or raw model inference (Anthropic, OpenAI). The value proposition crystallises as: replace, augment, or accelerate a knowledge-work activity at 30–80% the fully-loaded cost of a human FTE, with measurable throughput uplift.

Two production stacks coexist

  • Frontier model labs (Anthropic, OpenAI) produce general-purpose foundation models accessed via API, plus a thin consumer/enterprise UI layer. Output is tokenised inference sold at $3 / $15 per million input/output tokens for Claude Sonnet 4.5.
  • Application-layer agent platforms (Sierra, Decagon, Glean, Harvey, Cresta) produce a vertical-agnostic orchestration layer — agent runtime, retrieval/RAG, guardrails, observability, integrations — fine-tuned per customer on top of a foundation model API.

Margin structure — the defining constraint

ICONIQ's 2026 dataset puts the average AI-native product gross margin at 52% versus 75–85% for mature SaaS — a 23–33 point compression. Inference consumes ~23% of revenue at scaling-stage AI companies. Three concrete trajectories:

  • Anthropic inference gross margin: negative 2024 → 38% 2025 → mid-60s 2026, the steepest documented improvement curve in the category. Attribution: Bedrock distribution displacing direct-API serving costs, plus model efficiency gains (Anthropic spent ~4× less than OpenAI on training).
  • OpenAI gross margin: 33% Q1 2026; inference cost rose from $8.4B in 2025 to $14.1B projected 2026; operating margin −122% non-GAAP. Cash burn ~$27B 2026, projected $63B 2027.
  • Application-layer: best-in-class operators (Glean, Harvey) preserve 70–75% gross margins because the foundation-model lab absorbs the heaviest inference cost; Sierra's outcomes pricing internalises inference risk and produces gross margin closer to model-lab economics on heavy deployments.
Sources: Sacra company profiles for Anthropic, OpenAI; SemiAnalysis on Bedrock gross margins; SaaSMag ICONIQ 2026 dataset; TechTimes; Lorikeet on Sierra contract structure; Workativ on Glean pricing.
04The Replication Unit

The atomic unit differs sharply by sub-archetype: tokens served for model labs, enterprise environments for application platforms, vertical playbooks for specialised plays.

Portable versus HQ-resident assets

  • Portable (travels with each new unit): trained model weights, agent runtime/SDK, integration connectors, prompt/playbook library, observability dashboards.
  • HQ-bound: pre-training and RLHF infrastructure, alignment and safety team (Anthropic LTBT, OpenAI safety board), GPU procurement relationships with NVIDIA and hyperscalers, model evaluation harnesses, multi-tenant data isolation engineering.

Diseconomies of scale — three documented break-points

  • Inference cost exceeds resolution price. Production volumes reveal that agent architecture can be economically unviable at scale when LLM inference plus retrieval and orchestration overhead exceeds the human process being replaced. Pilots handling 200 test cases hit 3% failure → 300 failures/week at 10K volume → human-escalation cost spike.
  • Customer success org does not scale linearly with logo count. Sierra's 4–10 week onboarding creates a structural ceiling on logo additions; doubling revenue requires doubling solutions-architect headcount.
  • Model commoditisation. Inference cost per million tokens dropped ~80% from 2023 to 2025 — fatal for any platform whose moat depended on the arbitrage between API cost and customer price.
Sources: ChatForest analysis of Sierra Series E; Workativ on Glean implementation; VaaSBlock 2026 enterprise AI deployment gap report; IdeaProof on inference cost compression.

High ACV at the enterprise tier combined with usage-based NRR above 130% produces strong LTV/CAC — when the inference tax is contained.

COGS structure for application-layer platforms

  • Foundation-model API spend: 8–18% of revenue depending on pricing model. Pure usage/outcomes models (Sierra) absorb this directly; per-seat models (Glean) cap exposure via usage limits.
  • Inference infrastructure (own GPUs or reserved compute, if self-hosting fine-tuned model): 5–12% of revenue.
  • Hosting and non-GPU cloud: 3–6% of revenue.
  • Data and connector vendors (search indexes, telephony for voice agents): 1–4%.
  • Customer-success delivery (often booked as COGS for AI platforms with high implementation burden): 6–15%.

Stack-up: application platforms typically run 22–40% COGS, producing 60–78% gross margin. Frontier labs run 35–55% COGS in 2026, producing 45–65% inference gross margin.

Net revenue retention benchmarks

  • B2B SaaS median NRR 2026: 105–115%; best-in-class enterprise: 130%+.
  • Usage-based / consumption SaaS: 125% median, 140%+ top quartile.
  • Reference points: Snowflake ~160% annualised, Zoom ~130%, MongoDB ~120%.
  • AI-native platforms with usage components (Sierra, Anthropic API) plausibly sit in the 130–160% range given expansion through new use cases per logo.

Chief AI Officer is rising as a budget centre — Gartner expects 35% of CROs to have GenAI Ops on their team by 2026 — but the buying committee remains multi-stakeholder (procurement, security, legal, line-of-business).

Channels ranked by efficacy

  • 1. Inbound from foundation-model marketplaces — AWS Bedrock alone accounted for 30% of YoY AWS gross profit dollar growth in Q1 2026 while being 4% of AWS revenue; 100K+ Bedrock customers run Claude.
  • 2. Enterprise field sales / direct outbound — required for $500K+ ACV deals.
  • 3. System integrators (Accenture, Deloitte, Cognizant) — services pull-through for change management; Accenture invested in Cresta directly.
  • 4. Conferences / category events — SaaStr AI, Gartner Symposium, AWS re:Invent, Dreamforce; NeurIPS for talent recruiting more than sales.
  • 5. Content / SEO and thought leadership — model labs publish papers; application platforms publish ROI case studies.
  • 6. Referrals from logo customers — Sierra publicly cites Fortune 50 references as conversion accelerant.
  • 7. Hyperscaler co-sell — Microsoft+OpenAI, AWS+Anthropic — large customers buy through their existing enterprise agreement.
  • 8. PLG / community — for developer-facing products (Cursor, Anthropic Claude Code).

Sales cycle and pricing

  • API self-serve: same-day.
  • Mid-market: 60–120 days.
  • Enterprise: 6–9 months typical; regulated industries (healthcare, financial services) extend to 9–15 months due to security review, data-residency, and model-card approval.

Five live pricing models in 2026: per-token (Anthropic, OpenAI API), per-seat (Glean, ChatGPT Enterprise), per-agent / per-deployment (Decagon enterprise contracts), outcomes-based (Sierra, pioneered for AI in 2024), and hybrid platform-plus-usage (most enterprise contracts).

The pilot-to-production cliff

Only 11–14% of enterprise AI agent pilots reach production at scale. IDC reports 4 of 33 prototypes graduate. Top operators have mitigated this with outcomes-aligned commercial terms (Sierra), phased rollouts with explicit success-criteria gates, and embedded customer-success engineers acting as SI consultants on the platform's P&L.

08Risks and Failure Modes

Five failure modes recur in the documented record: foundation-model encroachment, inference cost compression, pilot cliff, reverse acqui-hire, and capital starvation in the non-frontier tier.

  • 1. Foundation-model encroachment. The model provider absorbs the use case. OpenAI's Operator, GPT Store, Tasks, Canvas, and Search cannibalised at least 200 funded GPT-wrapper startups in 2024 alone. 80% of AI startups are projected to fail by end-2026. OpenAI's own Operator product was shut down August 31, 2025 and absorbed into ChatGPT Agent — even the leader cannibalises its own products.
  • 2. Inference cost compression eliminates margin arbitrage. Token prices dropped ~80% from 2023 to 2025. Any business whose moat was margin between API cost and customer price evaporates.
  • 3. Pilot-to-production cliff. 86–89% of enterprise pilots fail to scale; reliability degrades at volume; unit economics flip negative when scaled token costs exceed human cost-per-task.
  • 4. Reverse acqui-hire. When a frontier-model relationship dries up or the product cannot differentiate, the cap-table-friendly outcome is a technology licence + team poach by a hyperscaler. Adept ($400M raised), Inflection ($1.5B+), Character.AI all followed this path. Investors recover capital but founder upside is capped.
  • 5. Capital starvation in the non-frontier tier. A handful of labs (OpenAI, Anthropic, Google DeepMind, xAI, Meta internal) can sustainably raise $10B+ tranches; the second tier (Cohere, Mistral, Reka) is structurally undercapitalised relative to compute requirements and faces strategic compression.

Documented failures and root causes

  • Adept AI (Amazon acqui-hire July 2024): raised $400M; failed to convert action-transformer demo into PMF; Amazon hired 80% of team plus licensed technology.
  • Inflection AI (Microsoft absorption March 2024): raised $1.5B+; consumer Pi lost to ChatGPT despite Reid Hoffman backing; Microsoft paid $650M and brought Mustafa Suleyman in as MS AI lead.
  • Character.AI (Google licensing deal August 2024): founders + 30 staff returned to Google.
  • Failed GPT wrappers (2024–25): 3,800 AI startups shut in 2025 (27% of 2024 cohort).

Preconditions for survival

To remain viable, an operator must achieve at least one of: distribution moat at enterprise (Sierra: F50 reference customers, multi-year contracts, brand-voice tuning sticky); data and integration moat (Glean's permissions-aware knowledge graph; Harvey's playbook library curated by ex-lawyers); foundation-model independence or vertical optimisation (own model layer, multi-model routing); capital scale to compete on training ($10B+ rounds with hyperscaler partnership); pricing model aligned with customer value (outcomes versus tokens passes risk to the operator that can engineer cost down).

Sources: IdeaProof AI startup failure tracker; getcoai on Adept reverse acquihire; Heavybit acqui-hire model analysis; VaaSBlock enterprise AI deployment 2026.
09When This Game Is Playable

The game requires either top-tier ML credentials or senior enterprise-buyer relationships (ideally both), proprietary distribution beyond cold outbound, and 5–8 years of cash-burn tolerance.

Required team capabilities

  • Frontier lab: 50–200 PhD-level researchers from OpenAI / DeepMind / FAIR alumni pool; senior alignment leadership; world-class infra / distributed-systems engineering (Anthropic's compute team includes ex-Google TPU and ex-OpenAI Triton leadership); experienced enterprise CRO hired by Series C.
  • Application platform: founder-CEO with operator credibility in the target buyer's world (Bret Taylor → CX/Salesforce; Harvey founders → BigLaw network; Cresta founders → Stanford ML + contact-center domain). Add 10–30 ML engineers, 20–50 forward-deployed engineers, an enterprise CRO at $5M ARR, and a security/compliance team by Series B.

Required capital base

TierTotal capital required to reach $100M ARR
Frontier model lab$5 – $20B+
Horizontal application platform (deep CS)$200 – $600M (Sierra ~$285M pre-Series E; Harvey ~$1B; Decagon ~$481M to $4.5B)
Vertical-horizontal (Glean)$400 – $800M

Required market conditions

  • Capital availability at $1B+ rounds (concentrated in ~15 funds: Sequoia, a16z, Founders Fund, Lightspeed, Coatue, Tiger, GV, Khosla, ICONIQ, Thrive, Wellington, T. Rowe Price, Fidelity, Bessemer, Greylock, GIC).
  • Foundation-model API availability with stable pricing trajectory.
  • Hyperscaler distribution channel (Bedrock, Azure AI Foundry, Vertex AI) for marketplace co-sell.
  • Enterprise budget shift from AI-experiment line items to core-operations line items (underway in CX, legal, support; lagging in regulated verticals).
  • Buyer willingness to enter outcomes-based or usage-based contracts.

Time horizon

  • Frontier lab: 7–12 years founder commitment to IPO/exit; Anthropic founded 2021, IPO-ready 2026 — but only with the founders' OpenAI alumni distribution head start.
  • Application platform: 4–7 years from founding to $1B+ EV. Sierra hit $15.8B in ~30 months, an outlier driven by Bret Taylor's distribution.
  • Median expectation for a non-celebrity founder: 6 years minimum.

Disqualifying conditions

  • No proprietary distribution channel beyond cold outbound.
  • Founding team lacks both top-tier ML credentials and senior enterprise-buyer relationships (need at least one; ideally both).
  • Total addressable budget per customer < $100K (SMB economics do not survive the implementation cost).
  • Use case fully covered by foundation-model native products (ChatGPT Tasks, Claude artifacts, GPT Store applets) — wrapper-only positioning.
  • Cannot defend gross margin > 50% within 24 months — capital starvation will follow as growth slows.
  • No path to bringing inference cost-per-transaction below human cost-per-transaction at production volume.
  • Founders unwilling to absorb 5–8 years of cash burn before FCF positive (Anthropic projects 2027 FCF+; OpenAI pushed breakeven to 2030).
