02 The Map of Business Processes Framework ▼

Every company on earth structures itself around three process layers. The framework is 20th-century consulting (variations of Porter's Value Chain and the McKinsey 7S enterprise model) and the macro layers have not changed with AI — only the entry points have.

The strategic insight

The core process (sales → sourcing → production → logistics → delivery) is unique to each vertical. The support processes (HR, IT, Finance, Legal) are universal — every business runs them the same way. All four support-function doors are open at SMB tier: Current, Shield, and Eudia occupy <0.15% of firms each at the top tier; the bottom 95% of firms by count are untouched.

Creative · 02 The 0.15% Reality

The "Empty Quadrant occupied" narrative collapses on the math. Current owns ~0.017% of US accounting firms. Shield owns ~0.02% of US MSPs. PE accounting platforms (Aprio, EisnerAmper, Citrin Cooperman, Current combined) own ~0.12%. Tier-1 platforms are competing for the top 100–200 firms in each vertical — by EBITDA, by ARR, by reputation. The other 119,860 accounting firms and 44,990 MSPs are structurally invisible to these players because their cost structures and acquisition pipelines do not scale below $1M EBITDA. Bobroff at $52M is sized exactly for the tier-2/3 segment they cannot reach.

Three reasons all four support-function verticals are viable simultaneously.

Reason 1 — One playbook, four deployments

HARP methodology (Section 7) is the same across HR, MSP, Accounting, and Legal: workflow decomposition, agent-human role split, capacity forecasting, governance layer. The connectors and savings calculators differ, but the playbook architecture does not. Marginal cost of vertical N+1 is the cell hiring cost (~$1.9M), not new R&D.

An HR client (HR firm serving 50 SMBs) acquired by Integration Co opens telemetry on 50 SMB tech stacks. An MSP client opens 200. An accounting client opens 300. A legal client opens 100. Current's blended cost-per-firm is roughly $17M (verified). Shield's MSP acquisitions cluster in the $3–8M EBITDA range. Bobroff's $1.5–3M target acquisition size lives exclusively in tier-2/3.

06 Financing Integration via Future Gainshare ▼

SMBs do not have $25K of discretionary cash. This blocked Accenture and McKinsey from SMB for fifty years. We resolve it by financing L1 against the L2 cash flow we ourselves will collect.

The sales pitch

"We run a 5-day audit at our cost. If you pass scoring, we deploy AI on your support function. You pay nothing upfront. We collect 25% of measured savings for 24 months, capped at $10K/month. When the contract ends, you keep the AI."

The four financing pathways — 2026 rates

Path A · Self-financed from L1 cash
Working capital from paying clients

L1 clients who pay upfront fund the L1 deployments of clients who deferred. Requires 30–40% upfront-pay mix. Low risk, no external capital. Cost of capital: 0%. Default model.

Path B · Revenue-based financing
Capchase / Pipe / Lighter Capital

L2 gainshare contracts are receivables. 2026 rates: Capchase Grow 10–12% flat over 3–24 months; Lighter Capital 2–8% of monthly revenue, cap 1.3×–1.5× funded amount; Pipe rates Not Publicly Disclosed but trading limits $25K–$100M. Effective APR equivalent: ~12–20%. Founderpath

Path C · Fund balance sheet loan
Working capital facility from Bobroff fund

The fund extends a working-capital facility to Integration Co secured against L2 contracts. Cleanest from cash perspective. SEC nuance: needs LPA-level disclosure under Section 206(2); arms-length pricing required. Effective rate set at fund-treasury cost + spread, ~8–10%.

Path D · B2B invoice factoring
Specialty B2B factor

Standard B2B factoring: 80–90% advance on invoice face value, factor fee 1.95–4.50% per 30-day period. Effective annualized cost 25–30%. Only justified for spike financing. PeerSense 2026

Blended working-capital cost — recommendation

Mix of Path A (60% of L1 cash needs) + Path B (25%) + Path C from fund (15%) yields blended cost ~7–9% APR on working capital. This is the assumption used in the Operating Roadmap (Section 16).

How we pre-qualify clients (scoring)

  1. Verifiable spend baseline on target support function — clean numbers to calculate gainshare.
  2. Owner-decision-maker present — no committee, no procurement office.
  3. Standard tech stack — Office 365, QuickBooks, ADP. Custom stacks double deployment cost.
  4. Vertical match — HR, HomeCare, MSP tier-2/3, Accounting tier-2/3, or Legal SMB.
  5. Acquisition optionality — owner 55+, no clear succession, or actively considering exit.
Creative · 07 The Audit-Fee Selection Pivot

Counter-intuitive verified insight: free audit converts at 10–20%; $25K paid audit converts at 25–45% (industry proxies from B2B SaaS funnels — RepVue, McKinsey-style assessment benchmarks). Charging for the audit IS the selection mechanism. Counter-instinct: making the audit free attracts tire-kickers who waste team capacity. Setting a $5K audit fee filters down to qualified leads while doubling conversion. Recommendation: $5K audit fee credited 100% against the L1 fee if the client proceeds. Effectively zero cost to a serious buyer, $5K cost to a casual one.

08 Section 206 Mechanics — What's Allowed, What's Blocked Deep dive ▼

Section 206 of the Investment Advisers Act of 1940 (15 U.S.C. § 80b-6) is the anti-fraud and fiduciary duty provision. It creates the boundary that incumbent venture and PE firms cannot cross — precisely where we want to operate.

What Section 206 actually prohibits

The 2023 Private Fund Adviser Rules — vacated

Status — June 2024

On June 5, 2024, the Fifth Circuit Court of Appeals vacated all five 2023 Private Fund Adviser Rules in National Association of Private Fund Managers v. SEC. The Court held the SEC exceeded its statutory authority under Section 206(4) and Section 211(h). The binding regime today is Section 206 anti-fraud + general fiduciary duty (SEC v. Capital Gains Research Bureau 1963) + the 2019 SEC Fiduciary Duty Interpretation (IA-5248) + customary side-letter disclosure practice. Morgan Lewis · SEC announcement

Bobroff at $52M = ERA, not RIA

Under Rule 203(m)-1, advisers solely to private funds with less than $150M regulatory AUM in the U.S. are exempt from full SEC registration but must file as Exempt Reporting Advisers (ERAs) via a partial Form ADV. Section 206 still applies to ERAs. But Rule 206(3) procedural requirements and many examination burdens do not. Bobroff is comfortably an ERA. 17 CFR § 275.203(m)-1

What counts as "affiliated"

  • Direct/indirect ownership ≥ 5% of voting securities → affiliate
  • 25% ownership creates presumption of control under Investment Company Act §2(a)(3)
  • If Bobroff fund/management company owns >25% of Integration Co → Integration Co is an affiliate → 206(3) applies when securities (warrants) flow between Integration Co and fund portfolio companies
  • If ownership stays <25% AND no other control indicia → not affiliated by presumption (but SEC still applies facts-and-circumstances test)

Why this blocks the incumbents

  • General Catalyst HATCo / Customer Value Capital — disclosed up front as part of fund thesis (proper LPA-level treatment). HATCo holds operating assets (Summa Health acquisition $485M) outside the standard 10-year fund cycle. Fierce Healthcare
  • Andreessen Horowitz Operating Network — operating partners are employees of a16z, services delivered free or below-cost to portfolio. Conflict disclosed in fund LPAs. a16z Team
  • Tiger Global — outsources to Bain & Co. and pays directly. No internal service team. Umbrex
  • Accenture / Deloitte / McKinsey / BCG / Bain — not RIAs under Section 206. Their constraint is audit-conflict rules (cannot hold equity in clients they advise) + cost structure ($400–$900/hour Big-4 senior, won't price for $25K SMB engagements).

10 Model B — Integration Co as Stand-Alone Startup (Fund-Backed) ▼

Integration Co is incorporated as an independent Delaware C-corp. The Bobroff fund is the largest investor (but <25% to stay outside affiliate presumption) with founder equity reserved for management, and room for strategic investors. Integration Co has its own board, cap table, and P&L.

Structure

Real-world precedent — KKR Capstone

Per KKR's SEC 10-Q disclosures (verbatim): "KKR Capstone is a group of entities owned and controlled by their senior management and is not a subsidiary or affiliate of KKR. KKR Capstone operates under several consulting agreements with KKR and uses the KKR name under license from KKR."

KKR explicitly structured Capstone to be not an affiliate of KKR — avoiding Section 206(3) and affiliate-transaction concerns. Capstone professionals are paid by Capstone (not KKR) and bill portfolio companies directly. 25+ years of operation; no SEC enforcement action against the structure. KKR 10-Q · Umbrex

Caveat: KKR initially listed Capstone as a subsidiary in 2011 annual report (later reversed). 2014 WSJ + Naked Capitalism coverage questioned whether legal form / economic substance gap is sustainable. No enforcement action followed. Substance-over-form risk exists; mitigated by genuine governance separation. Naked Capitalism

11 Recommendation — Which Legal Pathway Decision ▼
Working recommendation

Start as Model A (inside fund) for first 12 months; convert to Model B (stand-alone with fund as anchor sub-25% investor) at the proof-point of $3M ARR or first L3 acquisition completed.

Why this sequence

  • Model A speed wins early. Setting up an independent C-corp with board + SEC structure burns 3–6 months of legal work and $50–100K. Pre-revenue, Model B optionality is not worth the cost or delay.
  • The conversion path is well-trodden. Fund subsidiaries spinning out into stand-alone portfolio cos (with fund as anchor) is a standard mechanic. Counsel knows the playbook.
  • $3M ARR conversion gate is when talent grants matter for retention (we'll be competing with AI-startup comp), outside capital becomes accretive, and Eudia-style contingent capital ($30M + $75M) becomes available.
  • Section 206(3) risk is low in early days because Integration Co has zero portfolio company customers (fund has no acquisitions yet). The risk window opens precisely when we make first acquisition — which coincides with conversion trigger.
  • HATCo precedent confirms this pattern. General Catalyst created HATCo specifically because operating assets cannot live indefinitely inside a fund's 10-year cycle. The Summa Health acquisition required HATCo's permanent structure. Our equivalent: at first L3 conversion, Integration Co needs Model B.

What to lock in the original LPA

  1. Explicit disclosure of Integration Co as part of the fund thesis.
  2. Pre-approved spin-out language — at the fund's discretion, Integration Co may be reorganized as a stand-alone entity with the fund as anchor investor at sub-25% ownership.
  3. Fund-level priority on equity grants at conversion — fund LPs share in operator equity grants pro-rata to fund ownership.
  4. Independent valuation requirement on Integration Co-to-portco transactions from Day 1 — even if not strictly required at sub-$150M AUM.
  5. Annual disclosure schedule of warrant book, fee flows, and acquisition pipeline to all LPs — preempts Apollo/TZP enforcement risk.
  6. Authority for Bobroff fund to extend working-capital facility to Integration Co under stated arms-length pricing terms.

Questions to ask securities counsel before finalizing

  1. At Model A under sub-$150M ERA status, what disclosure is required for warrants Integration Co takes from non-portfolio companies?
  2. At Model A, can Integration Co charge fund portfolio companies for services without each transaction triggering 206(3) consent — or do we need blanket consent in the LPA?
  3. Under Model B, does Integration Co need to register as an investment adviser if it earns warrants in non-portfolio targets? (Likely no, but needs opinion.)
  4. Does the conversion from Model A to Model B trigger taxable event for fund LPs?
  5. What is the SEC's stance on Integration Co's warrant book — are warrants in non-portfolio cos considered "client assets" for advisor purposes?
  6. Can Integration Co's contingent-capital mechanic (Eudia-style $X upfront + $Y contingent on milestone acquisitions) be structured to satisfy both fund LPAs and independent board fiduciary duty post-conversion?
Creative · 11 The Two-Cycle Structure

General Catalyst figured out a key thing: an operating company that holds acquired SMBs cannot fit inside a single 10-year fund cycle, because the integration tail extends 10–15 years. Their solution was HATCo — a perpetual operating entity that the fund seeds but does not consume. Bobroff Integration Co should follow the same logic. The Bobroff fund's purpose is to invest. Integration Co's purpose is to operate. Two cycles, two structures, intersected at acquisition conversion gates. This is the explicit case for early conversion to Model B: Integration Co at L3 conversion needs the permanence Model B provides.

15 Playbook = Unit of Replication Deep dive ▼

The unit of replication is not the engineer, the customer, or the dollar of revenue. It is the playbook: a packaged answer to "what does a [vertical] firm look like in the age of AI." Playbooks scale because copying one playbook into a second firm is 10× cheaper than designing the first.

What is in a playbook

Section · 01
Target Org Chart

Augmented org chart for a typical firm in this vertical. Roles, headcount, agent placements. The "to-be" picture.

Section · 02
Workflow Library

15–25 atomic workflows that account for 80% of a firm's labor. Each broken into agent-step, human-step, hybrid-step.

Section · 03
Agent Stack

5–8 agents we deploy: which model, which tools, which APIs, which guardrails. Pre-built and tested.

Section · 04
Data Integration Recipes

Pre-built connectors for systems firms in this vertical use (ADP for HR, AlayaCare for HomeCare, Autotask for MSPs, QuickBooks for accounting, Clio for legal).

Section · 05
Savings Calculator

Pre-tested model: client headcount + spend + system list → 12-month gainshare forecast in 30 minutes.

Section · 06
Change-Management Script

30-day rollout plan. Who to talk to first, how to onboard staff, how to handle resistance. Non-technical half of integration.

Why playbook beats engineer as a unit

  • Engineers do not compound. 11th engineer adds 1× capacity. 11th playbook adds (capacity × all clients matching the playbook).
  • Customers are downstream of playbooks. A playbook unlocks an entire vertical. A customer is one engagement.
  • Playbooks are reusable IP. Sellable, licensable, defensible. An engineer can quit; a playbook stays.
  • Playbooks force quality. When the unit of replication is documented and reused, deficiencies surface immediately.

How we build a playbook

  1. Pilot 1 (1–2 firms): engineer team co-designs with real client. Discover the workflows. Draft playbook.
  2. Pilot 2–3 (3–5 firms): apply the draft. Identify deviations. Codify universal vs firm-specific. Cost per deployment drops 40%.
  3. General release: playbook V1 ships. New L1 uses playbook + 1 customizer day instead of 5 days bespoke.
  4. Quarterly refresh: telemetry refines the playbook. V1.1 captures workflow drift, new agent capabilities, edge cases.
Creative · 14 The Current Rebrand Lesson

Crete renamed itself to Current on June 2, 2026 — moving from "alliance of CPAs" to "single AI-native platform" branding. This is the post-roll-up integration phase signal. Phase 1: acquire firms under loose federation. Phase 2: integrate operations and consolidate brand. Phase 3: re-brand as single entity to extract platform multiples. Lesson for Bobroff: design the playbook with rebranding in mind from Day 1. Each acquired firm should run on the same HARP playbook (Phase 1), gradually consolidate ops (Phase 2), then re-brand to "Bobroff HomeCare" or "Bobroff Insurance" (Phase 3) for exit. Current's move tells us how the playbook's life-cycle ends.
